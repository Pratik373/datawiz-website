**Q1.** She was taken _____ by the unexpected kindness shown by her neighbours.
- A) off
- B) aback
- C) apart
- D) over

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** "Taken aback" is a fixed idiom meaning to be surprised or shocked by something unexpected. "Taken off" means to remove or start, "taken apart" means disassembled, and "taken over" means to assume control — none of these fit the context.

**Q2.** The scientist's findings were so _____ that they overturned decades of accepted theory.
- A) mundane
- B) redundant
- C) groundbreaking
- D) tentative

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** "Groundbreaking" means pioneering or revolutionary, which fits a discovery that overturns established theory. "Mundane" means ordinary, "redundant" means unnecessary/repetitive, and "tentative" means uncertain — all opposite to what's needed here.

**Q3.** Choose the correctly punctuated sentence:
- A) The teacher said, "students must submit their work by friday."
- B) The teacher said "Students must submit their work by Friday."
- C) The teacher said, "Students must submit their work by Friday."
- D) the teacher said, "Students must submit their work by Friday."

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** After a reporting verb followed by a comma, the quoted speech begins with a capital letter. "Friday" is a proper noun and must be capitalised. The sentence itself must begin with a capital "T" (The teacher). Only option C satisfies all these rules.

**Q4.** Identify the error: "The committee comprising of five experts have submitted its report."
- A) The committee comprising
- B) of five experts
- C) have submitted
- D) its report

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** "The committee" is a collective noun treated as singular in formal writing, so the verb should be "has submitted," not "have submitted." Additionally, "comprising of" is incorrect — "comprise" does not take "of" (it should be "comprising five experts"), but among the given options, the subject-verb agreement error in option C is the primary grammatical mistake being tested.

**Q5.** Spot the error: "Neither the manager nor the employees was informed about the change."
- A) Neither the manager
- B) nor the employees
- C) was informed
- D) about the change

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** With "neither…nor," the verb agrees with the noun closer to it (proximity rule). Here, "employees" is the nearest subject and is plural, so the verb must be "were informed," not "was informed."

**Q6.** Improve the sentence: "The accused was proven innocent beyond any reasonable doubt."
- A) The accused was proved innocent beyond any reasonable doubt.
- B) The accused was proven innocently beyond any reasonable doubt.
- C) The accused had proven innocent beyond any reasonable doubt.
- D) No improvement needed.

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** In formal and legal English, "proved" is the preferred past participle of "prove." While "proven" is acceptable in informal American English, "proved" is considered grammatically precise. "Innocently" (option B) is an adverb incorrectly replacing the adjective "innocent," and option C uses an incorrect tense construction.

- A) for

- C) over
- D) against

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** "Speak about" is the correct prepositional phrase when discussing a topic. "Speak for" means to advocate for someone, "speak over" is not a standard collocation in this context, and "speak against" means to oppose — none of these match the intended meaning.

**Q8.** The delegation was _____ by the Prime Minister himself, signalling the importance of the summit.
- A) led
- B) leaded
- C) lead
- D) leaden

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** "Led" is the past tense and past participle of "lead." "Leaded" is not a standard verb form, "lead" (as present tense) would be grammatically incorrect in this passive construction, and "leaden" is an adjective meaning heavy or dull.

**Q9.** Change to Indirect Speech: He said, "I will finish the project by tomorrow."
- A) He said that he will finish the project by the next day.
- B) He said that he would finish the project by the next day.
- C) He said that he would finish the project by tomorrow.
- D) He told that he would finish the project by the next day.

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** In indirect speech, "will" changes to "would" (backshift of tense), and "tomorrow" changes to "the next day." Also, "said" (without an object) is used for indirect speech without a specific listener, so "said that" is correct, not "told that" (which requires an object like "told him that").

**Q10.** Choose the correct sentence:

**Explanation:** "Smarter" is already the comparative form of "smart." Adding "more" creates a double comparative error ("more smarter"), which is grammatically incorrect. "Most smartest" is a double superlative. Option B uses the correct simple comparative.

**Q14.** A man invests ₹12,000 at 5% per annum simple interest. After how many years will the interest equal ₹3,600?
- A) 4 years
- B) 5 years
- C) 6 years
- D) 8 years

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** SI = (P × R × T) / 100 → 3600 = (12000 × 5 × T) / 100 → 3600 = 600T → T = 6 years.

- B) A pointer that points to memory that has been freed
- C) A pointer that holds a very large address
- D) A pointer that is never initialised

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** A dangling pointer is one that points to memory that has already been freed (deallocated) or that belongs to a variable that has gone out of scope. **Q64.** What is the time complexity of inserting an element at the beginning of a singly linked list?

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Inserting at the beginning of a singly linked list requires creating a new node and making it point to the current head, then updating the head pointer. This is a constant-time operation — O(1) — regardless of the list size.

**Q65.** Which of the following traversal methods visits nodes in the order: Left → Root → Right?
- A) Preorder
- B) Inorder
- C) Postorder
- D) Level order

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Inorder traversal visits nodes in Left → Root → Right order. Preorder is Root → Left → Right. Postorder is Left → Right → Root. Level order visits nodes level by level from top to bottom.

**Q66.** A stack uses which principle?
- A) FIFO
- B) LILO
- C) LIFO
- D) FILO is different from LIFO

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** A stack follows the LIFO (Last In First Out) principle — the element inserted last is the first to be removed. Note that FILO (First In Last Out) is logically equivalent to LIFO, but the standard term is LIFO. FIFO (First In First Out) is the principle used by queues.

**Q67.** Which data structure is best suited for implementing a recursive function call mechanism?
- A) Queue
- B) Stack
- C) Array
- D) Hash Table

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Function calls use the "call stack" — each function call pushes a stack frame (with return address, local variables) onto the stack, and when the function returns, the frame is popped. Recursion relies entirely on this stack mechanism.

**Q68.** What is the worst-case time complexity of Quick Sort?
- A) O(n log n)
- B) O(n²)
- C) O(n)
- D) O(log n)

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Quick Sort's worst case is O(n²), occurring when the pivot chosen is always the smallest or largest element (e.g., on already sorted or reverse-sorted arrays with a naive pivot strategy). Average case is O(n log n). With randomised pivot selection, worst case is extremely unlikely.

**Q69.** In a Max-Heap, the root element is always:
- A) The smallest element
- B) The largest element
- C) A random element
- D) The median element

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** In a Max-Heap, the parent node is always greater than or equal to its children. Therefore, the root is always the largest element in the heap. In a Min-Heap, the root is always the smallest.

**Q70.** Which of the following is NOT a balanced binary search tree?
- A) AVL Tree
- B) Red-Black Tree
- C) B-Tree
- D) Binary Search Tree

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** AVL trees, Red-Black trees, and B-trees are all self-balancing data structures. A plain Binary Search Tree (BST) is not self-balancing — it can become skewed (degenerate) leading to O(n) operations in the worst case.

**Q71.** What is the space complexity of a hash table with n elements?
- A) O(1)
- B) O(log n)
- C) O(n)
- D) O(n²)

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** A hash table needs to store n key-value pairs. Even with a load factor, the space used is proportional to n — making the space complexity O(n). The actual memory is O(n) where n is the number of elements stored.

### Part 8: Object Oriented Programming — C++ (Q72–Q81)

**Q72.** What is the output?
```cpp
class Base {
public:
    virtual void show() { cout << "Base"; }
};
class Derived : public Base {
public:
    void show() { cout << "Derived"; }
};
int main() {
    Base *b = new Derived();
    b->show();
}
```
- A) Base
- B) Derived
- C) Base Derived
- D) Compile error

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Since `show()` is declared `virtual` in the Base class, the call `b->show()` is resolved at runtime (dynamic dispatch). Even though `b` is a `Base*` pointer, it points to a `Derived` object, so `Derived::show()` is called — printing "Derived."

**Q73.** Which of the following is an example of runtime polymorphism in C++?
- A) Function overloading
- B) Operator overloading
- C) Template functions
- D) Virtual function overriding

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** Runtime polymorphism (also called late binding or dynamic dispatch) in C++ is achieved through virtual function overriding. The function to call is determined at runtime based on the object type. Function overloading, operator overloading, and templates are all resolved at compile time (compile-time polymorphism).

**Q74.** What is the purpose of a copy constructor in C++?
- A) To destroy an object
- B) To create a new object as a copy of an existing object
- C) To overload the = operator
- D) To allocate memory dynamically

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** A copy constructor creates a new object by copying the state of an existing object of the same class. It is called when an object is passed by value, returned by value, or explicitly copied. It is different from the assignment operator (=) overloading, which copies into an already-existing object.

**Q75.** What does the keyword `protected` mean in C++?
- A) Members are accessible only within the class
- B) Members are accessible everywhere
- C) Members are accessible within the class and derived classes
- D) Members cannot be inherited

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** `protected` access specifier makes members accessible within the class itself and within classes derived from it, but not from outside the class hierarchy. `private` restricts to the class only; `public` allows access everywhere.

**Q76.** In C++, which of the following is true about multiple inheritance?
- A) C++ does not support multiple inheritance
- B) A class can inherit from more than one base class
- C) Multiple inheritance is only possible with interfaces
- D) Multiple inheritance requires virtual keyword

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** C++ supports multiple inheritance — a derived class can inherit from more than one base class. This can lead to the "diamond problem" when two parent classes share a common ancestor, which is resolved using virtual inheritance. Java/C# use interfaces to simulate this, but C++ directly supports multiple base classes.

**Q77.** What is the output?
```cpp
class A {
public:
    A() { cout << "A "; }
    ~A() { cout << "~A "; }
};
int main() {
    A obj1, obj2;
}
```
- A) A A ~A ~A
- B) A ~A A ~A
- C) A A ~A
- D) ~A ~A A A

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Constructors are called in order of declaration: obj1 constructed first (prints "A"), then obj2 (prints "A"). Destructors are called in reverse order (LIFO): obj2 destroyed first (prints "~A"), then obj1 (prints "~A"). Output: A A ~A ~A.

**Q78.** Which concept of OOP ensures that internal details of an object are hidden and only necessary information is exposed?
- A) Inheritance
- B) Polymorphism
- C) Abstraction
- D) Encapsulation

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Abstraction hides the implementation complexity and exposes only the essential features/interface. Encapsulation is about bundling data and methods together and restricting direct access (data hiding). While related, abstraction focuses on "what an object does" and encapsulation on "how it is implemented and protected."

**Q79.** What is the result of calling a pure virtual function in C++?
- A) It calls the base class implementation
- B) It results in a compile-time error if the class is instantiated
- C) It calls the derived class implementation at compile time
- D) It does nothing

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** A pure virtual function (`virtual void func() = 0;`) has no body in the base class, making that class abstract. Any class with an unimplemented pure virtual function cannot be instantiated — attempting to do so causes a compile-time error. The derived class must override all pure virtual functions to be concrete.

**Q80.** Which operator is used for dynamic memory allocation in C++?

- B) alloc
- C) new
- D) create

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** In C++, the `new` operator is used for dynamic memory allocation on the heap. It also calls the constructor. The corresponding deallocation operator is `delete`. `malloc()` is a C function that can also be used in C++ but does not call constructors.

**Q81.** What is operator overloading in C++?

- B) Defining a new type of operator
- C) Inheriting operators from a base class
- D) Using operators in recursive functions

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Operator overloading allows existing operators (like +, -, *, ==) to be redefined for user-defined types (classes/structs) so they perform meaningful operations on those objects. For example, overloading `+` for a `Complex` class allows adding two Complex objects using `c1 + c2`.

### Part 9: Operating Systems (Q82–Q86)

**Q82.** What is "thrashing" in operating systems?
- A) A type of sorting algorithm
- B) When the CPU spends more time swapping pages than executing processes
- C) A method of disk defragmentation
- D) When multiple processes compete for CPU time

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Thrashing occurs when the degree of multiprogramming is too high and the OS spends most of its time swapping pages in and out of memory rather than executing processes. It severely degrades system performance. It is prevented by reducing the number of concurrent processes or increasing RAM.

---

**Q83.** Which scheduling algorithm is known as "Shortest Remaining Time First (SRTF)"?
- A) FCFS
- B) Round Robin
- C) Priority Scheduling
- D) Preemptive SJF

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** SRTF (Shortest Remaining Time First) is the preemptive version of SJF (Shortest Job First) scheduling. It preempts the current process if a new process arrives with a shorter remaining burst time. It gives optimal (minimum) average waiting time.

---

**Q84.** What is a "race condition" in operating systems?
- A) A competition between processes for the CPU
- B) A situation where the outcome depends on the timing/order of process execution
- C) When two processes have the same priority
- D) A deadlock involving only two processes

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** A race condition occurs when multiple processes access and manipulate shared data concurrently, and the final result depends on the particular order of execution. It leads to inconsistent/unpredictable outcomes and is prevented using synchronisation mechanisms like mutexes and semaphores.

---

**Q85.** In the context of memory management, what is "fragmentation"?
- A) Division of a program into multiple threads
- B) Wasted memory space that cannot be used efficiently
- C) Division of the CPU time between processes
- D) A method of compressing files

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Fragmentation refers to wasted memory that cannot be effectively used. External fragmentation occurs when free memory is scattered in small blocks (in segmentation); internal fragmentation occurs when allocated blocks are larger than needed (in paging/fixed partitioning).

**Q86.** Which system call is used to create a new process in Unix/Linux?
- A) exec()
- B) create()
- C) fork()
- D) spawn()

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** `fork()` is the Unix/Linux system call that creates a new process (child process) by duplicating the calling process (parent). The child gets a copy of the parent's address space. After `fork()`, `exec()` is often called to replace the child's image with a new program.
